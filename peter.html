Peter Vance

Units of Sound



Peter Vance formulates processes into which an input - be it a piece of text, an image or a sound - can be transformed into a new sensory output. The ordinary or overlooked is presented to the viewer as something new – something that is to be contemplated.

Please can you describe your initial idea for the work?

A lot of my art really seeks not to express, or make grand points. When I’m making it I am only concerned with the investigation of the creative process. I became interested in the question: “Are there methods that can remove my authorship?” I started to look at ways in which the viewer is able to understand the process and the effect of the artwork without an introductory block of text, or any other authorial imprinting, involved. I want the viewer to have a moment of joy when they work out what’s going on within the artwork; the same moment of wonder that I had when the machine I designed finally worked!

Units of Sound

How does the work connect to the theme of language and creativity?



Language is really fascinating as a bunch of weird noises. When I speak, I force air into my mouth, emitting a sound that pops a similar idea in someone else’s head. What the Units of Sound machine does is detach language from its meaning, dismantling everyday communication to unleash the raw sounds. In this way, the machinations of language come to light. I wanted Units of Sound to be very simple to understand. It shows how it works and what is happening. I don’t want to use technology in obscure ways, I’m interested in using very simple technology rather than a “magic box”, like phones or computers. In this way the viewer can really be part of the process and concoct their own meanings.

How does the work relate to the Lye Valley site?


I first discovered the beautiful escape of the Lye Valley during lockdown. Places like the Lye Valley force you to find creative solutions, a call for experimenting that I was very happy to respond to. In a sense, the wilderness and practical limitations of the Lye Valley resonate with my practice deeply. I’ve always had the innate desire to find ways to turn one thing into another, setting things in motion to see what happens. In other words: to play. I think there’s value in finding new ways to represent something. For instance, I’ve now recreated Units of Sound cyanotypes in jesmonite, a sturdier material for outdoor exhibiting. I would have never engaged in that creative process if it wasn’t for the Lye Valley!
