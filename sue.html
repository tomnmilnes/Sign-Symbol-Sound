Exchanges



Sue Chamberlin is an Oxfordshire based contemporary artist, educator and chartered landscape architect who has been working in environmental and design practices for 20 years. Through her mixed media artworks, Chamberlin looks at the different ways we all experience the environment, exploring everyday landscapes, liminal edge-lands and the unnoticed, through collected objects, drawings and recordings.

Please can you describe your initial idea for the work?

In the Lye Valley, I found that I was overwhelmed by the extraordinary amounts of communication occurring around me. The ‘Wood Wide Web’ is the name given to the mycorrhizal network that allows communication between all trees, plants and fungi. I decided to reduce these complex systems down to three fundamental types of activity, and these communications formed the basis for three sound works, or ’Exchanges’. Exchange 1 imagines the communications of a tree under attack, and others helping it. Exchange 2 theorizes how the sharing of resources amongst the community might sound. Exchange 3 tells of an intimate and intertwined conversation.


Exchanges


How does the work connect to the theme of language and creativity?


With this work, I wanted to make tangible a language we are as yet unable to understand or depict. The communications I have imagined are governed by the Fibonacci sequence - a mathematical structure that appears similarly in everything from nature to computers, and is reflected in the musical elements of my Exchanges. There is a connection between the concrete structures of my sound works, and the environment of the Lye Valley. I used the Fibonacci sequence as a tangible framework to restrict this potentially very abstract sound work.

How does the work relate to the Lye Valley site?

This work would not exist had the Lye Valley not inspired me with its wealth of communication on display. I have always been fascinated by the complex systems that are going on under our feet, and in the Lye Valley I had an urge to ‘tap in’ and listen to the conversations. I imagined the trees as periscopes allowing me a glimpse into this world, and so I envisioned connecting via the wires of a telephone as a merging between human and nature’s methods of communication. All of the sounds in the Exchanges are collected directly from the Lye Valley, accompanied by wind instruments played by me. I see myself as a facilitator of these communications from the natural world to ours, rather than the orchestrator.
