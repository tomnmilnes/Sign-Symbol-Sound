The House of Hours



Isis Amelie Hjorth is currently fascinated by orthography as part of her MA art practice at Oxford Brookes University. A former journalist and playwright, Hjorth is also a postdoctoral researcher at The University of Oxford, studying participatory digital tools for artistic production in developing nations, where she has spent time working in Sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia.

Please can you describe your initial idea for the work?

Letters are symbols; the smallest functional units in written language. I’ve had a soft spot for silent letters—the silent ‘H’, in particular—since the early 2000s. They illustrate the common mis-correspondence between symbol and sound in ortography. ​​I set out to use that silent ‘H’ to communicate key aspects of language formed by various linguistic theories and my lived experience, relating to a sense of being silenced, perhaps. In the act of transcending orthography, the silent ‘H’ in ‘The House of Hours’ takes on a new form beyond language. It dissolves to form a ‘House of Ours’, a communal space. An imaginary place for us, the audience.

The House of Hours




How does the work connect to the theme of language and creativity?



Language and creativity—and cognition for that matter—are inextricably intertwined. Fundamentally, this work evokes the poetic function of language; it's metaphorical, about connotations. In the work, I explicitly play with the starkly differing connotations of the words “hours” and “ours”. To some degree, that poetic function of language equals creativity, a creativity I hail as a means of engaging in tiny, subversive acts of rebellion against the production and use of texts by increasingly intelligent algorithms. Embracing the poetic function of language enables us to contribute towards networks of intertextuality that aren’t easily parse-able by neural networks, trying to distil or extract human meaning at scale from digital corpora of texts.

How does the work connect to the Lye Valley site?

Orthography and the literary devices employed by ‘The House of Hours’ are human-centric. The homophones ‘hours” and ‘ours' are signifiers of key human organising principles: time and ownership. This became clear when I brought the house to the Lye Valley. I couldn't help but laugh a little. Its awkward presence sparked a juxtaposition between 'house' and 'habitat'. We barely acknowledge trees and plants as sentient beings; the complexity of their ways of communication is mostly beyond our grasp. So the work relates to the site by alluding to the fundamentally contrasting communication and organisation paradigms of non-human environments and ecosystems and that of their human counterparts.
