DNA



BARRY MARTYNSKI originally studied Fine Art but spent his career working in historic building conservation, before a recent diagnosis of Huntington's Disease prompted a return to his art. Barry likes to work with vibrant form and colour to explore themes of 'internal landscapes', helping him to come to terms with issues around loss of identity and communication.

Please can you describe your initial idea for the work?

The painting is called DNA which reflects my interest in genetics following a recent diagnosis of Huntington’s Disease (HD), a hereditary neurological condition. Over time, the science of genetics has become woven into my life and my art, especially the way it can be seen as a language in itself and as a form of interpretation. The notions of chance and luck also interest me as I had a 50-50 chance of inheriting the disease, so I created my work by using and playing with random colour combinations to create new shades, as well as tapping into the Surrealist practise of following an ‘inner stimulus’.

DNA

How does the work connect to the theme of language and creativity?



Having HD affects me in many ways, including my ability to speak and communicate. Making art is a tool for me to express myself in ways that are increasingly difficult to do through speech. The cell-like structures relate to my interest in genetics and DNA, which is the code or language of our bodies. I have spent many hours considering the gene-editing tools being used by specialist surgeons, in relation to my condition. I’m also interested in the idea of the palimpsest, where layers build to obscure the original form or expression although traces can still be seen; a metaphor for my own condition.

How does the work relate to the Lye Valley site?


The overlapping themes I mentioned above are punctuated through my painting and are central elements of my work, which can be felt or sensed, as internal landscapes or emotional echoes, across time. The bright colours popping out against the wide expanse of a cloud-streaked late summer sky visually creates a meaningful collaboration between my painting DNA and Lye Valley as a site. I can also see that the structure and shapes of my painting contrast with the wilder, more natural surrounds of the Lye Valley.
